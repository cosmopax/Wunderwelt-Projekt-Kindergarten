Wunderwelt Projekt Kindergarten

REIMAGINING URBAN EXISTENCE

The Future is Grown, Not Built.

Wunderwelt is a practical framework for urban resilience. We organize neighborhoods into 150-person nodes‚Äîthe optimal scale for trust and real cooperation. Our focus is actionable autarky: growing food locally, managing community microgrids, and running shared fab labs. We replace dependency with capability. Not a daycare, but a hands-on workshop for living better.

Sociology

Dunbar's Nodes

Limiting social units to 150 active peers prevents the cognitive overload of mega-cities. Research shows this scale maximizes trust and collective action, essential for managing common-pool resources without coercive bureaucracy.

Action: Structural capping of residential clusters at 150 members.
Psychology

Biophilic Interventions

Integrating fractal patterns and pervasive greenery to lower cortisol by up to 40%. Cognitive function is optimized through "Attention Restoration Theory," utilizing natural stimuli to reset focus.

Action: 40% vertical greenery mandate & fractal architectural facades.
Political Economy

Liquid Democracy & Gifting

Moving beyond transactionism. Liquid Democracy allows citizens to delegate expertise-based votes in real-time. This "Gift of Knowledge" powers a circular economy where social capital is the primary currency.

Action: Blockchain-based resource allocation & expertise "Gifting" protocols.
Cybernetics

Radical Autarky

Closed-loop nutrient cycles. Vertical farming provides 80% of caloric needs, while solar glass and waste-to-energy systems ensure zero-grid dependency.

Action: District-level aquaponics & nutrient recovery labs.
Anthropology

Infinite Play

Urban spaces designed for non-zero-sum interactions. Replacing transit-only corridors with multi-functional "Play Zones" that foster spontaneous cooperation and ritualized bonding.

Action: Reclamation of 60% of road surface for community use.
Sociology & Safety

Defensible Space Theory

Oscar Newman's concept that residential designs can reduce crime and enhance safety by creating spaces where residents can naturally monitor and take ownership of their environment. This complements Dunbar's Number by ensuring that the 150-person nodes have clear boundaries and natural surveillance points.

Action: Design streets and public areas with natural surveillance points, territorial reinforcement, and symbolic barriers that encourage resident stewardship while maintaining openness.

    Dunbar, R. I. M. (1992)

    Neocortex size as a constraint on group size in primates. Journal of Human Evolution, 22(6), 469-493.

    Establishes the cognitive limit of stable social relationships (~150 people) that influences community design principles.

    DOI: 10.1016/0047-2484(92)90081-J

    Kellert, S.R. et al. (2008)

    Biophilia hypothesis. Island Press.

    Explores the human affinity for natural systems and how biophilic design improves cognitive function and reduces stress.

    DOI: 10.5822/978-1-59726-147-7

    Newman, O. (1972)

    Defensible Space: Crime Prevention Through Urban Design. Macmillan.

    Foundational work on how physical design affects social control and community safety.

    ISBN: 978-0026420702

    Ostrom, E. (1990)

    Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action. Cambridge University Press.

    Analysis of how communities can successfully manage shared resources without top-down control.

    DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511807763

    Benkler, Y. (2006)

    The Wealth of Networks: How Social Production Transforms Markets and Freedom. Yale University Press.

    Examines how networked cooperation can create value outside traditional market mechanisms.

    ISBN: 978-0300125771

    Raworth, K. (2017)

    Doughnut Economics: Seven Ways to Think Like a 21st-Century Economist. Chelsea Green Publishing.

    Framework for economic systems that operate within planetary boundaries while meeting human needs.
